Active problems - priority order
P1 - UrgentWarning bites
ContextsNail cutting, coat blowing, lead removal at bedtime, dangerous object guarding.

Training wins
Whistle recall
Only ever used for recall, with the fish treats. It once called her off a dog she was reacting to through a fence. We practise off-lead recall in the enclosed area behind the basketball courts at the Strand.
Parallel walking
Rhea walks her alongside other dogs at the enclosed field. Calm, low-key time near dogs without it tipping over into over-excitement.
Ball and tug reinstated
Working well post-heat without the arousal problems previously associated with them.
Impulse control - don't take what you're not handed
Teaching her not to take things she isn't handed. We walked around for a week with a cloth hanging out of a pocket, and have now graduated to a piece of worn (smelly) clothing. She can sniff it, but the moment her mouth opens it is the pen for 5 minutes. The same person who puts her in should take her out without fanfare, and she still needs the "break" command to be released.
Heel walking improving
Still needs a lot of treats but much better. Say "good girl" repeatedly while she is in range and not pulling, and "Yes" the moment she looks up at you, rewarding immediately. If she pulls ahead, stop and turn the other way ("this way") and circle until she locks back in. If she won't lock in she may be over-stimulated, so squat down until she re-engages. No hellos on walks for now. You can ask for a sit with light lead tension near a trigger and reward. Whenever she is calm and looking at a trigger, count to 3, say "yes", and reward.
Leave it game
Hold a low-value treat in your hand and show it to her. If she tries to grab it, close your hand. Whenever she leaves it, mark "Yes" and reward with a higher-value treat from the other hand (kept behind your back). When she is leaving it consistently, place the treat on the floor with your hand over the top, treating whenever she leaves it and covering it when she doesn't. She should never get the treat, or anything, she has been asked to leave.
Collar grab desensitisation
Training her to accept being taken by the collar, paired with treats, so a collar hold is a good thing rather than a trigger.
Running with Kaya (started)
Now running with her regularly, just 200m up and down the road, 2x a week, with great success. Building toward canicross.
Longer-term goals

Canicross
Now running 200m 2x a week with success (see wins). Building distance toward a full canicross setup as lead manners and recall firm up.
Reliable off-lead
Currently practised in enclosed areas only. Whistle recall building the foundation.
