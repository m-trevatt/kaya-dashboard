Warning bites & guarding Urgent

The pattern

Kaya guards specific stolen or self-discovered objects, not food or space. When she grabs something and someone tries to take it directly, she escalates through warning bites. She has learned that biting makes the unwanted thing stop, so every time it works it reinforces the behaviour. This pattern is spreading to new contexts: nail cutting, coat blowing, lead removal at bedtime, and dangerous object guarding.

Core rule: do not take items from her while she is guarding. Anything that is not life-threatening stays with her. Follow the guarding protocol.

Bite incidents

12 June 2026: A dog sitter removed her own cardigan and wrapped it around her waist while walking Kaya. Kaya grabbed the cardigan. The sitter tried to keep hold of it, triggering the guarding response. Warning bites on the sitter's forearm, breaking skin for the first time.

25 June 2026: A different sitter followed the crate release protocol. Kaya grabbed one of the sitter's trainers. The sitter tried to pull it from her directly rather than using the guarding protocol. Kaya escalated to jumping and grabbing at clothing, tearing it, and made contact with the sitter's upper arm. Skin was not broken.

Both incidents happened because the person tried to take something from her. When the protocol is followed, these situations resolve calmly.

Arousal & reactivity Elevated baseline

How she works

Kaya's arousal baseline is higher than normal for her age, confirmed by the trainer. Her first real heat (finished 12 June 2026) brought it down a little, but it is still high. If a stimulus reflects energy back at her, she escalates immediately. Calm dog means very excited but manageable. High-energy dog means she matches and doubles it. Same with people.

Other dogs

If the other dog is calm, she is mostly well behaved but very excited. This excitement can mask underlying anxiety. If the other dog is high energy, she matches and at least doubles it. Parallel walking at the enclosed dog field is helping her stay calmer around other dogs. The whistle recall once pulled her off a dog she was reacting to through a fence.

People

Same energy-mirroring pattern. Follow the greeting protocol (ignore completely, hands away, release from a sit). Visible progress. Pre-walk animation is frustration-based, not positive excitement.

Management

We practise engage-disengage outside regularly. The main thing is staying calm ourselves, because she settles when we settle. Copying what the trainer does in person has been the most useful thing.

Ball and tug are back since the heat, without the over-excitement they used to cause.

Build trust: praise her a lot and don't be scared of her. She wants to do the right thing desperately, she just needs to know when she is doing it.

Fear

Minimal. One fear response to a sail being dropped quickly at the front of the house. No issues with fireworks, thunder, being alone, or new environments. Car travel settles eventually. Handling generally fine. Cats, squirrels, and small animals are untested.

Impulse control: don't take what you're not handed

Teaching her not to take things she isn't handed. We walked around for a week with a cloth hanging out of a pocket, and have now graduated to a piece of worn (smelly) clothing. She can sniff it, but the moment her mouth opens it is the pen for 5 minutes. The same person who puts her in should take her out without fanfare, and she still needs the "break" command to be released.

Leave it game

Hold a low-value treat in your hand and show it to her. If she tries to grab it, close your hand. Whenever she leaves it, mark "Yes" and reward with a higher-value treat from the other hand (kept behind your back). When she is leaving it consistently, place the treat on the floor with your hand over the top, treating whenever she leaves it and covering it when she doesn't. She should never get the treat, or anything, she has been asked to leave.

Collar grab desensitisation

Training her to accept being taken by the collar, paired with treats, so a collar hold is a good thing rather than a trigger.

On-walk management

Heel walking

Still needs a lot of treats but much better. Say "good girl" repeatedly while she is in range and not pulling, and "Yes" the moment she looks up at you, rewarding immediately. If she pulls ahead, stop and turn the other way ("this way") and circle until she locks back in. If she won't lock in she may be over-stimulated, so squat down until she re-engages.

No hellos on walks for now. You can ask for a sit with light lead tension near a trigger and reward. Whenever she is calm and looking at a trigger, count to 3, say "yes", and reward.

Triggers on the street

Cross the road or stick to one side when another dog or person approaches. Sometimes put in a sit, sometimes distraction treats straight on the nose.

Foot-planting: if caught too close to a trigger (someone coming out of an alleyway), she plants her feet and no amount of pulling will move her. Ask the person to move on, encourage Kaya to stay on all fours.

After a trigger passes, sometimes follows behind at distance. Engage-disengage: whenever she is looking at a trigger, she will come to you for praise. Just say "good girl" and give her pats. If she is locked on and won't look away, cue "Yes" and throw a treat the other way.

Walking setup

No Halti currently. Retractable lead. Heel walk to the park, full 5 metres at the park for sniffing, released with a sit-wait then "break" or "go sniff". The Strand in mornings, lower footfall, better for threshold management.

Foxtails

There are currently many foxtails in all the standard walking areas. Avoid them. Her long fur hides them easily, and you need to check all four legs when you get home. Stick to the paved areas around the Strand, or take her the other way through the forest tunnel (expert handlers only).
